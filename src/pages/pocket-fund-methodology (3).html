Phase 01

Mandate Definition

Most acquisitions fail before the first conversation is ever made. Not in diligence. Not at the negotiation table. They fail the moment a buyer starts searching without knowing precisely what they're looking for.

You've seen it. Months of deal flow that goes nowhere. Opportunities that feel right until they don't. Capital sitting idle while you evaluate things that were never going to fit. That's not a sourcing problem — that's a mandate problem. Nobody bills you for the time you waste on the wrong deals.

We fix it first. The buyers who move fastest and waste least are the ones who started with the clearest brief — not a vague sector preference, not a rough budget range. A precise, documented mandate that tells you exactly what fits and exactly what doesn't.

For Borderless — generating $2–3M ARR, heading into a Series A — the mandate wasn't "find a business to acquire." It was: identify £50K–£250K in annualised cashflow through structures the market had never seen, before the fundraise window closed. At a 20× revenue multiple, every £50K added to their topline was worth £1M in enterprise value. Without issuing a single new share.

For the Dino Games client — 20+ years of operating experience, fresh PE exit — we ran structured exposure across four categories before recommending a direction. Criteria emerged from observation, not assumption. By the time sourcing began, we weren't guessing at what fit. We'd already seen what didn't.

"The mandate we build with you becomes the lens everything else gets evaluated through. It's the reason we can move quickly when the right deal appears — and walk away cleanly when it doesn't."

For SmartPrompt, we explored four categories before submitting a single LOI. That process produced a filter precise enough to disqualify two serious candidates mid-engagement — not because the businesses were bad, but because they didn't fit what had been defined at the start.

The best deals aren't listed anywhere. The operators who built them aren't looking to sell — until the right conversation finds them.

The opportunities on Acquire.com, Empire Flippers, or any brokered marketplace have already been seen by everyone. The price reflects that. The seller has been coached on their multiple. The information asymmetry — the thing that makes a deal genuinely attractive — is gone before you arrive.

The deals worth doing are the ones nobody else has found yet. Every universe we build starts from zero — primary research, proprietary data, channels that require actual effort to access.

"For Borderless, we built a target universe that had never existed before — mapping a market that had never been systematically approached for M&A."

For Dino Games — scraping infrastructure built to pull App Store and Google Play data across thousands of titles before contacting a single founder. Phase 03

Targeted Outreach

There's a reason most outreach doesn't work. It's built for volume, not for trust. And in every market we operate in, trust is the only currency that opens doors.

The first thing most sourcing operations do is launch a campaign. Thousands of emails, templated messaging, broad targeting. We tried that. Email response rate came in under 5%. Not because the targets were wrong — because the channel was wrong. Within a week, we'd shifted everything to Reddit and Twitter.

Response rates jumped to 20–30% immediately. By week three, 35–40% — and three LOIs issued in the same week. The difference wasn't volume. Every message referenced something specific: a product decision the founder had made, a feature they'd built, something posted publicly. Not a template. A real opening to a real conversation.

"Personalisation was treated as a first-order variable — not an optimisation layer. Each message was a real opening to a real conversation."

For Borderless — a market that ran on relationships, where cold transactional approaches had failed every previous attempt — we ran every channel simultaneously and measured conversion in real time.

"Every conversation that doesn't convert is still an asset. By the time we complete an engagement, we've mapped how an entire market thinks about their businesses, their capacity, and their appetite for exits."

That intelligence informed every negotiation that followed. Walking in knowing more about the counterparty's situation than they expect you to — that's the edge outreach at this scale produces.

Phase 04

Diligence & Structuring

You're about to spend six figures on a business you've never operated. The seller is friendly. The numbers look clean. The story makes sense. But the version of the business you're being shown is almost never the complete version.

Not because sellers are dishonest. Because most sellers don't have a full picture of their own financials either. An accountant categorised something one way three years ago and never revisited it. A database field set up with logic that quietly became wrong. A payment route from an old partnership that never got turned off. None of it shows up on a P&L summary. All of it becomes your problem the moment you sign.

This is not a worst-case scenario. This is a normal deal. The only way to find what's actually there is to go to the source — every processor, every account, every transaction — and reconstruct the picture yourself.

Phase 05

Execution & Close

Getting to LOI is not the hard part. Staying disciplined from LOI to close — while a seller is pushing for speed, stakeholders are misaligned, and momentum is working against you — that's where most deals quietly fall apart.

We've closed across five asset classes. What execution required in each wasn't just paperwork — it was the discipline to hold structure when it was easier to move fast, to refuse unsafe mechanics when a seller pushed for them, and to ensure the buyer was operational the moment the deal closed.
